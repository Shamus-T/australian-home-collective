## Purpose

The Retail Knowledge Base captures practical buying, delivery, installation and ownership issues observed in real purchasing decisions.

It exists to help Australian Home Collective produce guides and tools grounded in genuine consumer problems rather than specifications or keyword research alone.

It is not a source of employer-specific information, confidential material, unverified anecdotes or universal claims. Every entry must still be checked against current public information before publication.

## Entry standard

Each entry should record:

- ID and category;
- issue or commonly overlooked consideration;
- why it matters;
- reader-facing planning advice;
- evidence or verification needed;
- suitable articles, tools or downloads; and
- status and review date.

Use neutral language such as `commonly overlooked` rather than blaming customers, retailers, delivery teams or installers.

## Appliances — refrigeration

### RKB-001 — The cavity is measured, but the delivery route is not

**Issue:** A refrigerator may fit the intended cavity but not pass from the delivery vehicle to the kitchen.

**Why it matters:** Gates, security doors, internal doors, hallways, tight bends, stairs, landings, handrails and islands can become the true constraint.

**Planning advice:** Measure the complete delivery route at its narrowest and lowest points, not only the final space. Record width, height and turning space separately.

**Verification needed:** Product and packaged dimensions; delivery-provider requirements; site-specific assessment for difficult access.

**Suitable for:** Fridge guides, Fridge Fit & Delivery Planner, printable delivery-path checklist.

### RKB-002 — Packaged and unpackaged dimensions differ

**Issue:** Packaging can materially increase width, height or depth.

**Why it matters:** An appliance may pass an opening unpackaged but not in its carton. A delivery provider may have rules about when or where packaging can be removed.

**Planning advice:** Check current packaged dimensions where access is restricted and confirm the delivery provider's process.

**Suitable for:** Fridge and freezer guides, delivery planner.

### RKB-003 — Width and depth must both be considered

**Issue:** Turning a refrigerator sideways does not necessarily create a narrower profile.

**Why it matters:** Some appliances are nearly square in plan; others may be effectively larger because of doors, hinges, handles or packaging. The limiting orientation can change at a corner.

**Planning advice:** Record width and depth separately for packaged, unpackaged and manufacturer-approved removable configurations. Do not assume a side-first approach will pass.

**Suitable for:** Delivery planner logic and corner explanations.

### RKB-004 — Delivery teams cannot overcome every physical or safety constraint

**Issue:** Some buyers assume a delivery team can always manoeuvre an appliance into place.

**Why it matters:** Restricted or unsafe access may prevent delivery or installation as planned. Policies, equipment and site assessments vary by provider.

**Planning advice:** Treat access as part of the purchase decision. Confirm difficult routes with the delivery provider before ordering.

**Publication wording:** Avoid universal claims about work health and safety law or a particular delivery outcome. Explain that physical constraints, site conditions and provider requirements may limit what can be done.

**Suitable for:** Fridge planner, appliance delivery guides, printable checklist.

### RKB-005 — Ventilation clearances are not optional dimensions

**Issue:** The appliance body fits, but manufacturer clearances have not been allowed.

**Why it matters:** Insufficient clearance may affect performance, efficiency, heat management or compliance with installation instructions.

**Planning advice:** Compare the cavity with the current model-specific clearance requirements, not only the appliance dimensions.

**Suitable for:** Refrigeration guides and cavity assessment.

### RKB-006 — Doors, drawers and bins need operating clearance

**Issue:** A refrigerator fits the cavity but nearby walls, benches or handles restrict door opening or internal drawer removal.

**Why it matters:** A nominal 90-degree opening may not provide the access required to remove shelves or bins. Door thickness and hinge geometry can matter.

**Planning advice:** Check the manufacturer's door-opening diagrams and allow for adjacent walls, cabinetry and handles.

**Suitable for:** Fridge planner, kitchen-planning guides.

### RKB-007 — Services and projections reduce usable depth

**Issue:** Power points, water connections, skirting, pipes or uneven walls occupy space behind the appliance.

**Why it matters:** The refrigerator may sit farther forward than expected or place pressure on a connection.

**Planning advice:** Measure to the most restrictive projection and check service-location instructions before selecting a model.

**Suitable for:** Fridge planner and cavity checklist.

### RKB-008 — Fridge doors or handles may be removable, but this cannot be assumed

**Issue:** Buyers rely on a reduced dimension without confirming that removal is permitted and available for that model or delivery service.

**Why it matters:** Door removal can require manufacturer procedures, tools or a technician, and some components may not be intended for routine removal.

**Planning advice:** Use reduced dimensions only when supported by current manufacturer information and confirmed with the delivery provider.

**Suitable for:** Fridge planner and delivery guide.

## Appliances — dishwashing and laundry

### RKB-101 — Adjacent cupboard handles can block a dishwasher door

**Issue:** A dishwasher fits its opening but its door collides with a projecting handle on adjacent cabinetry.

**Why it matters:** The dishwasher door may not open fully, preventing normal loading or basket movement even after installation is complete.

**Planning advice:** Check the full door arc against adjacent doors, drawers, handles, walls and kickboards before choosing or installing the appliance.

**Suitable for:** Dishwasher buying guide, kitchen fit checklist, future appliance-clearance tool.

### RKB-102 — Service locations can prevent an appliance sitting correctly

**Issue:** Power, water or waste connections are positioned directly behind an appliance with insufficient recess space.

**Why it matters:** Hoses, plugs or valves can increase required depth, become crushed or make servicing difficult.

**Planning advice:** Check the appliance's installation diagram and plan services in an accessible, permitted location.

**Suitable for:** Dishwasher and laundry guides. Electrical and plumbing work must be referred to appropriately qualified tradespeople.

### RKB-103 — Laundry doors and appliance doors can collide

**Issue:** Cabinet, room or neighbouring appliance doors obstruct the washer or dryer door.

**Why it matters:** Access may be uncomfortable or prevent loading, cleaning or filter removal.

**Planning advice:** Draw or physically mark each door's full swing and allow space for the user and laundry basket.

**Suitable for:** Laundry planner and washer/dryer guides.

### RKB-104 — Filter and maintenance access is forgotten

**Issue:** The appliance fits, but routine filters, pumps, drain points or service panels cannot be accessed easily.

**Why it matters:** Maintenance becomes difficult and water may need to be managed at floor level.

**Planning advice:** Check the manual for routine access points and leave usable working room.

**Suitable for:** Laundry guides and checklists.

### RKB-105 — Stacking requires model-specific compatibility

**Issue:** A dryer is assumed to stack on a washing machine without checking the approved kit and dimensions.

**Why it matters:** Compatibility, stability, controls and service access vary.

**Planning advice:** Confirm the current manufacturer-approved stacking arrangement for both models.

**Suitable for:** Laundry guides and planner.

## Televisions and wall mounting

### RKB-201 — Screen size is only the first wall-mount decision

**Issue:** Planning stops after choosing the television size.

**Why it matters:** Viewing height, wall construction, fixing points, bracket movement, power, data, antenna, soundbar and cable paths affect the installation.

**Planning advice:** Treat wall mounting as a coordinated layout and services plan.

**Suitable for:** TV Wall-Mount Planner and wall-mount guide.

### RKB-202 — Televisions are commonly mounted too high

**Issue:** The screen is centred visually on the wall without considering the seated viewing position.

**Why it matters:** A high screen can create an uncomfortable viewing angle, particularly for long sessions.

**Planning advice:** Plan around the primary seated eye level, furniture and any necessary compromise. Treat generic formulas as guidance rather than a universal rule.

**Suitable for:** TV planner and size/height guide.

### RKB-203 — Stud positions may not match the desired screen centre

**Issue:** The desired position is selected before fixing locations are known.

**Why it matters:** Gyprock or plasterboard walls generally require an appropriate structural fixing strategy. Bracket width and lateral adjustment affect centring.

**Planning advice:** Identify the wall construction and suitable fixing points before selecting the bracket. Structural suitability should be confirmed by an appropriately qualified installer where uncertain.

**Suitable for:** TV planner and bracket guide.

### RKB-204 — Cable concealment must be planned before mounting

**Issue:** Power, antenna, data, HDMI, optical and future cable routes are considered after the television is on the wall.

**Why it matters:** Ports may be inaccessible and visible cabling may be difficult to correct. Fixed electrical work has legal and safety requirements.

**Planning advice:** Plan current and future connections, conduit or draw wire, and component locations in advance. Refer fixed electrical work to a licensed electrician.

**Suitable for:** TV planner, cable-planning guide.

### RKB-205 — Power-point design and location affect bracket clearance

**Issue:** A standard or recessed power point conflicts with the bracket, television body or plug.

**Why it matters:** Low-profile mounts leave limited space and rear-facing connectors may be difficult to access.

**Planning advice:** Coordinate the outlet, bracket rails, VESA mounting points and cable bend radius before installation.

**Suitable for:** TV planner. Do not provide electrical-installation instructions.

### RKB-206 — Bracket type changes wall load and cable movement

**Issue:** Flat, tilting and full-motion brackets are treated as interchangeable.

**Why it matters:** Projection, leverage, articulation, wall fixing, port access and cable travel differ substantially.

**Planning advice:** Compare the television's size, weight and VESA pattern with the bracket rating, wall type and required movement.

**Suitable for:** Bracket comparison guide and TV planner.

### RKB-207 — Price alone is a poor bracket comparison

**Issue:** A bracket is chosen only because it is cheap or premium.

**Why it matters:** Relevant differences can include load documentation, adjustment, sag, locking, levelling, cable management, included fixings and support.

**Planning advice:** Assess compatibility, construction, adjustment and documentation rather than assuming all inexpensive brackets are unsafe or all premium brackets are suitable.

**Suitable for:** Bracket guide and recommendation methodology examples.

### RKB-208 — Soundbar placement changes the layout

**Issue:** The soundbar is added after screen height and cabling are fixed.

**Why it matters:** It may block the television's receiver, require upward-firing clearance, need separate power or move with a full-motion screen.

**Planning advice:** Record soundbar dimensions, mounting method, load, cable path and relationship to the screen before fixing positions.

**Suitable for:** TV planner and soundbar-placement guide.

### RKB-209 — Surge protection and connected equipment are part of the plan

**Issue:** Planning covers the screen but not the power and connected equipment arrangement.

**Why it matters:** Consoles, media players, network equipment and sound systems require accessible, ventilated positions and a deliberate power strategy.

**Planning advice:** Plan the whole system and seek current authoritative electrical guidance where needed.

**Suitable for:** TV planner and home-entertainment guide.

## Furniture and room planning

### RKB-301 — Furniture fits the room but not the route

**Issue:** A sofa, table or cabinet is selected using room dimensions alone.

**Why it matters:** Doorways, lifts, stair turns and landings may prevent delivery.

**Planning advice:** Measure the item in realistic orientations and the complete path, including removable components only when confirmed.

**Suitable for:** Furniture Fit Planner and delivery checklist.

### RKB-302 — Dining furniture leaves inadequate circulation

**Issue:** Table dimensions are checked without allowing for occupied chairs and walkways.

**Why it matters:** A technically fitting table may make the room difficult to use.

**Planning advice:** Plan the table, pulled-out chairs and main circulation routes together.

**Suitable for:** Dining planner and table guide.

### RKB-303 — Recliners require movement clearance

**Issue:** A recliner is positioned using its upright footprint.

**Why it matters:** The extended mechanism may hit a wall, table or walkway.

**Planning advice:** Check the full operating envelope, including wall-hugger requirements where applicable.

**Suitable for:** Living-room guides and furniture planner.

### RKB-304 — Rug size should be assessed with the furniture layout

**Issue:** A rug is selected in isolation.

**Why it matters:** The relationship between rug edges, furniture legs and circulation affects how cohesive and practical the layout feels.

**Planning advice:** Mark the proposed dimensions on the floor or use a calibrated visualiser before buying.

**Suitable for:** Rug visualiser and living-room guides.

## Universal ownership considerations

### RKB-901 — After-sales support can distinguish similar products

**Issue:** Two products have similar specifications, but support after purchase is not considered.

**Why it matters:** Service coverage, response channels and parts availability can affect the ownership experience when a problem occurs.

**Planning advice:** Ask who provides support, how it is contacted, whether service reaches the reader's postcode and how parts or common wear items are obtained.

**Publication boundary:** Teach questions to ask. Avoid broad negative claims about a manufacturer without strong, current evidence.

**Suitable for:** Major-appliance guides and recommendation methodology.

### RKB-902 — Written warranty and consumer guarantees are different considerations

**Issue:** A voluntary warranty is treated as the complete statement of a consumer's rights.

**Why it matters:** Australian Consumer Law includes automatic consumer guarantees separate from voluntary warranties.

**Planning advice:** Keep any explanation general and refer readers to the ACCC's [consumer rights and guarantees](https://www.accc.gov.au/consumers/buying-products-and-services/consumer-rights-and-guarantees) guidance.

**Publication boundary:** Do not provide legal advice, determine individual entitlements or offer generic judgements about optional care plans or extended warranties.

**Suitable for:** Ownership sections and an authoritative resources page.

### RKB-903 — Maintenance access is part of fit

**Issue:** A product can be installed but not conveniently cleaned, filtered, drained or serviced.

**Why it matters:** Routine ownership tasks may require more clearance than normal operation.

**Planning advice:** Check current manuals for maintenance points and the space required to reach them.

**Suitable for:** Most appliance and furniture categories.

### RKB-904 — Future household needs affect suitability

**Issue:** The choice meets today's need without considering a realistic near-term change.

**Why it matters:** Moving, growing households, accessibility needs or room changes may shorten the useful life of the purchase.

**Planning advice:** Consider likely needs over the expected ownership period without overbuying for unlikely scenarios.

**Suitable for:** Buying frameworks and `Our Take` sections.
