# Australian Home Collective Humanisation Guide

Use this guide when writing or revising public copy. It sits alongside the editorial blueprint and applies to headings, introductions, cards, category pages and guide bodies.

## Aim

Write like an experienced Australian homeowner explaining what has proved useful, what tends to cause trouble and what may not be worth buying.

The copy should feel considered and specific. It should not sound as though every topic has been passed through the same template.

## Voice

- Use Australian English.
- Prefer plain sentences over polished-sounding slogans.
- Name the actual household problem instead of calling something practical, useful, simple or easy.
- Let the room, routine or decision shape the article structure.
- Keep cautions where they matter, but do not qualify every sentence.
- Recommend doing without a product when that is genuinely the better answer.
- Use contractions sparingly where they make the sentence sound more natural.

## Hyphens and compound adjectives

Hyphens are not banned. Keep them where they prevent ambiguity or are part of an established term. The problem is repetition.

Prefer:

- "a system that is easy to manage" over "an easy-to-manage system"
- "a cupboard where things are easy to find" over "a well-organised cupboard"
- "products you use every day" over "daily-use products"
- "an option that does not require permanent changes" over "a renter-friendly option"
- "works in a small room" over "small-space friendly"

Use established terms such as "under-sink", "built-in" and "open-plan" only when they are the clearest wording.

## Phrases to watch

Do not automatically use:

- "This guide explains..."
- "The useful starting point..."
- "Start with..."
- "What to check before buying..."
- "Common mistakes to avoid"
- "Our take"
- "Final thoughts"
- "practical", "simple", "useful", "easy" or "everyday" as filler
- "It is not about X. It is about Y."
- tidy three-part lists when the subject does not naturally have three parts

These phrases can still appear when they are genuinely the clearest choice. They should not define the site voice.

## Openings

Open with a recognisable situation, a useful observation or a direct answer. Do not spend three paragraphs restating the title.

Good openings may:

- describe the moment the problem becomes noticeable
- explain why common advice fails in a particular kind of home
- give the main recommendation immediately
- contrast two real household situations
- begin with the decision the reader is trying to make

## Headings and structure

Use headings that belong to the topic. A laundry article, pet article and outdoor article should not all move through the same sequence.

Not every guide needs sections called "Start with", "What to check", "Common mistakes" and "Final thoughts". Use checklists where the reader needs a checklist, comparisons where choices need comparing, and room walkthroughs where placement matters.

## Editing test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Could this paragraph appear unchanged in another category?
2. Does the sentence name something concrete, or is it built from reassuring adjectives?
3. Have hyphenated compounds begun to stack up?
4. Does the introduction add information beyond the title?
5. Are cautions proportionate to the risk?
6. Does the ending give the reader a useful conclusion rather than repeat the article?

If the copy could belong anywhere on the site, make it more specific.
