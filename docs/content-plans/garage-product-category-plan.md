# Garage Product Category Plan

## Purpose

This document maps future Garage product categories for Australian Home Collective guides and later product sections.

No product links are being added yet. No affiliate links are being added yet. No prices are being added yet. No product testing claims should be made unless genuine testing has actually happened and can be described clearly.

The Garage cluster should support the site's before-you-buy strategy. Future Garage content should help readers measure, sort, check access, understand storage limits and avoid poor purchases before buying shelves, tubs, hooks, racks, cabinets or wall systems.

Garage product sections should support practical guidance rather than replace it. Readers should still get value from a guide even if no commercial links are present or clicked.

## Garage content strategy

Garage content should focus on practical checks for Australian homes, townhouses, units, rentals and shared household storage areas.

The Garage cluster's role is to help readers:

- measure available floor, wall and ceiling-adjacent space before buying storage
- sort items before buying more containers, shelving, hooks or wall systems
- check access to doors, vehicles, walkways, tools, garden gear and household overflow
- consider load, wall type, floor level, moisture, pests, heat and rental limits
- avoid overbuying storage tubs, shelving, hooks or racks before the garage has been edited
- keep storage choices tied to real routines rather than making every visible gap into storage

Garage articles should stay practical and cautious. The aim is not to design or certify a garage storage system. The aim is to help readers notice fit, access, suitability and risk before buying.

## Garage safety and wording rules

Garage storage has higher safety risk than kitchen or laundry storage because it can involve heavy loads, tools, chemicals, ladders, bikes, wall fixing, ceiling storage, moisture, pests, batteries, sharp items and child/pet access.

Internal wording rules:

- Avoid structural advice.
- Avoid electrical advice.
- Avoid building advice.
- Avoid safety-certification advice.
- Do not tell readers that a wall, ceiling, shelf, hook, rack or fixing is safe.
- Refer readers to manufacturer guidance, load ratings, correct installation instructions and qualified trades where needed.
- Mention landlord or body corporate approval where drilling, fixing, ceiling storage or permanent changes may be involved.
- Be careful with chemicals, fuels, pesticides, batteries, sharp tools, power tools, ladders and heavy items.
- Do not imply that all garages are suitable for drilling, ceiling storage, wall-mounted systems or heavy shelving.
- Avoid saying that a product prevents accidents, keeps children or pets safe, or suits every home.
- Use cautious wording such as "may suit", "may help", "check first", "if appropriate for the space" and "follow manufacturer guidance".

## Product category risk levels

### Low-risk categories

Low-risk Garage categories usually involve simple, non-permanent storage that is easy to move and does not rely on heavy loads, wall fixing or safety-sensitive placement.

Examples:

- simple storage tubs
- clear boxes
- light-duty bins
- small parts organisers
- freestanding lightweight shelves for low-load items
- labels and sorting systems

Handle these with normal before-you-buy checks. Avoid saying that containers will solve clutter on their own, and remind readers to sort items before buying more tubs.

### Medium-risk categories

Medium-risk Garage categories may involve weight, access, moisture, stability, rental surfaces, sharp items or chemical-adjacent storage, but they do not necessarily require structural mounting.

Examples:

- freestanding shelving
- tool cabinets
- pegboards
- wall rails for light tools
- garden tool racks
- sports gear storage
- workbench storage
- renter-friendly garage systems

Use cautious wording around load, stability, access, fixing, surface suitability and household use. Encourage readers to check product instructions, load ratings and rental or body corporate requirements where relevant.

### High-risk / caution categories

High-risk Garage categories involve heavy loads, overhead storage, wall or ceiling fixing, chemicals, electrical charging, ladder storage, bike hanging, fuel, batteries or child/pet access.

Examples:

- ceiling-mounted storage
- wall-mounted heavy storage
- heavy-duty shelving
- ladder hanging systems
- bike hooks and hoists
- chemical cabinets
- fuel or battery storage
- electrical tool charging areas
- anything requiring structural fixing

These categories need cautious, limited language. Do not imply that a setup is safe because it looks tidy. Do not give installation instructions. Public content should focus on questions to check, measurements to take, and reminders to follow manufacturer guidance or seek qualified help where needed.

## Future Garage product categories

### 1. Freestanding garage shelving

What the category covers:

Freestanding shelving units for boxes, household overflow, camping gear, seasonal items, tools, garden supplies and general garage storage.

Good fit for:

Households with clear floor space, items that need visible storage, renters who need non-permanent storage, and garages where wall mounting is unsuitable.

What to measure/check before buying:

- shelf width, depth and height
- ceiling height
- floor space and walkway clearance
- garage door and car door clearance
- shelf load rating
- item weight and size
- floor level and stability
- moisture, dust and pest exposure
- whether shelves need wall anchoring or other fixing

Common mistakes:

- buying shelves before sorting stored items
- choosing shelves too deep for a narrow garage
- placing heavy items too high
- ignoring load ratings
- blocking vehicle access, doors or walkways
- assuming freestanding means suitable for every surface

Risk level:

Medium.

Safe wording notes for future public content:

Use measurement-led language. Refer readers to product load ratings and manufacturer instructions. Avoid saying shelving is safe for all garages or all loads.

## Categories to treat carefully or avoid

These categories need extra caution and should not be casually recommended:

- ceiling-mounted storage
- wall-mounted heavy storage
- chemical cabinets
- fuel or battery storage
- electrical tool charging stations
- child-accessible tool or chemical storage
- ladder and bike hanging systems
- anything requiring structural fixing

Future public content should not make installation, structural, electrical, chemical-safety or child/pet safety claims. If a category depends on wall, ceiling, load, electrical or chemical suitability, the guide should keep advice to measurement and suitability questions, then point readers to manufacturer guidance, product labels, approval requirements or qualified trades where needed.
