## Purpose

This framework keeps editorial, commercial and product decisions consistent with Australian Home Collective's mission as the publication grows.

Not every opportunity should be accepted. Significant proposals should be assessed deliberately before work or commercial commitments begin.

## Decision sequence

### 1. Does it align with the mission?

Will it help Australians make better home-planning or buying decisions? If not, stop.

### 2. Does it solve a real problem?

The proposal should address a genuine question, planning challenge or commonly overlooked consideration. Search demand may support a decision, but it is not sufficient on its own.

### 3. Does it build trust?

Consider whether it will improve reader confidence, strengthen the publication's reputation and remain defensible if scrutinised publicly.

### 4. Does it strengthen the publication?

Prefer resources with lasting value. Consider the maintenance burden as well as the initial benefit.

### 5. Is it commercially responsible?

Revenue should support the publication rather than define it. Ask whether the work would still be useful and editorially justified without a commission or sponsorship.

### 6. Does it meet the publishing standards?

The proposal must comply with the Editorial Charter, Editorial Style Guide, Recommendation Methodology and relevant risk or tool standards.

### 7. Can its risks and limitations be managed honestly?

Do not proceed if the proposal requires false certainty, unsupported claims or a level of legal, safety or technical assurance the publication cannot provide.

## Decision record

For significant proposals, record:

| Criterion | Assessment |
| --- | --- |
| Solves a real problem | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Improves planning | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Builds trust | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Has lasting value | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Commercially responsible | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Meets editorial standards | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Risks can be managed | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Suitable for Australian readers | Yes / No / Needs work |
| Maintenance is realistic | Yes / No / Needs work |

A `No` or `Needs work` outcome requires revision, explicit acceptance of a manageable trade-off, or rejection. The matrix is a decision aid, not a substitute for judgement.

## Applying the framework

### New articles

- Does the article fill a genuine gap or add practical value?
- Does it contain information beyond a generic specification summary?
- Is the intended reader and decision clear?
- Can the claims be verified and maintained?

### New planning tools

- Does the tool reduce uncertainty without promising certainty?
- Are its inputs, assumptions and limitations understandable?
- Could a reasonable user misread the result as an approval or guarantee?
- Can the tool be tested and maintained across common devices?
- Does the risk level justify development?

### Affiliate partnerships

- Is the partner relevant to the publication and its readers?
- Would the content remain useful without the commercial relationship?
- Can editorial independence and clear disclosure be maintained?
- Could the relationship undermine reader trust?

### Sponsored content

- Is the sponsorship clearly identifiable?
- Does the work still meet all editorial standards?
- Is the topic genuinely useful to readers?
- Has the sponsor been prevented from controlling independent conclusions?

### Advertising

- Is the advertising proportionate and clearly distinguishable from editorial content?
- Does it materially damage readability, privacy, accessibility or performance?
- Is the revenue worth the effect on trust and experience?

### New categories

- Does the category naturally fit home planning?
- Can Australian Home Collective add distinctive practical value?
- Is there enough depth to serve readers well?
- Will expansion dilute the publication's identity or current quality?

## Default principles

When uncertain, choose:

- accuracy over speed;
- quality over quantity;
- clarity over complexity;
- usefulness over popularity;
- honest limitation over false confidence; and
- trust over revenue.

## Final question

> **Will this make Australian Home Collective a better publication?**

If the answer is not a confident yes, reconsider the proposal.
