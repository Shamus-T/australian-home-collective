## 1. Purpose

Australian Home Collective helps Australians make better home-planning and buying decisions before they spend money.

We do not tell readers what to buy. We explain what to measure, compare, plan and consider so they can make informed choices for their own homes, needs and budgets.

Every article should reduce uncertainty and the risk of buyer's remorse.

## 2. Editorial philosophy

Every guide must:

- solve a genuine problem;
- improve a planning or buying decision;
- explain relevant trade-offs honestly;
- acknowledge limitations;
- contain practical value beyond product specifications; and
- remain useful for years or have a realistic review plan.

Search demand can help prioritise useful work. It is not, by itself, a reason to publish.

## 3. Voice

The publication voice is:

- calm;
- practical;
- honest;
- clear;
- professional;
- respectful; and
- recognisably Australian without forced slang.

Readers should feel that a knowledgeable person is explaining what they would want their own family to understand before spending money.

## 4. Tone

Explain rather than sell. Guide rather than command.

Avoid:

> You should buy a full-motion bracket.

Prefer:

> A full-motion bracket may suit rooms where the screen needs to face more than one seating area.

Use firm language when safety, a verified requirement or an unavoidable physical constraint justifies it. Do not weaken accurate safety guidance merely to sound conversational.

## 5. Australian English

Use Australian spelling and usage consistently, including:

- colour;
- metre and millimetre;
- organisation;
- maximise;
- behaviour;
- licence (noun) and license (verb); and
- travelling.

Use metric units first. Include imperial measurements only when the product category commonly uses them, such as television screen sizes, and provide metric context where useful.

Use `Australian Consumer Law`, `ACCC`, `power point`, `gyprock or plasterboard` and other terms in ways Australian readers will recognise. Explain regional or trade terminology when ambiguity is possible.

### Hyphens and compressed compounds

Use hyphens only when they prevent a genuine ambiguity or are part of an established term. Australian Home Collective should not sound as though several nouns and modifiers have been compressed into one label.

Prefer ordinary phrasing:

- `a washer and dryer placed next to each other`, not `a side-by-side washer-dryer layout`;
- `storage under the sink`, not repeated use of `under-sink storage`;
- `a room that serves as both a bathroom and laundry`, not `a bathroom-laundry combination`;
- `requirements for that model`, not `model-specific requirements`; and
- `long term` when it follows the noun, while retaining `long-term` only where it is needed before a noun.

Common open compounds should remain open, including `living room`, `home office`, `washing machine`, `power point`, `heat pump dryer`, `top loader` and `front loader`.

Do not build sentences around strings of hyphenated modifiers. If a phrase needs two or more hyphens to work, rewrite the sentence in plain language unless the term is a recognised name or quotation. URLs and existing slugs are not changed to match prose style.

## 6. Audience

Write for Australians who may own, rent, build, renovate, downsize, move, furnish rooms or buy appliances.

Do not assume technical knowledge. Do not talk down to readers. Explain specialised terms at first use and make the practical consequence clear.

## 7. Article purpose

Before drafting, identify:

- the decision the reader is trying to make;
- the measurements or facts that materially affect it;
- common assumptions or overlooked constraints;
- the trade-offs between reasonable options; and
- the checks required before purchase, delivery or installation.

## 8. Article structure

A typical guide may include:

1. A direct introduction to the planning problem
2. Why the issue matters
3. Measurements and constraints
4. Options and trade-offs
5. Commonly overlooked considerations
6. Ownership and support considerations
7. `Our Take`, when a concise editorial synthesis adds value
8. A `Before You Buy` checklist
9. Related guides or planning tools

This is a flexible model, not a mandatory template. Do not add thin sections to satisfy a structure.

## 9. Introductions

Open with the practical consequence, decision or familiar situation. Avoid generic throat-clearing.

Avoid:

> Buying a television can be difficult because there are many options.

Prefer:

> A television that looks modest in a showroom can dominate a small lounge room, while the same screen may feel undersized on a wide wall. Measuring the space and viewing distance first can prevent an expensive mismatch.

## 10. Headings

Headings should help readers scan for decisions and answers.

Prefer specific headings such as:

- `What should you measure before buying?`
- `Will the doors open beside a wall?`
- `What happens if the studs do not align?`

Use noun headings when they are genuinely clearer. Do not turn every heading into a question mechanically.

Use one page-level H1. Nest H2 and H3 headings logically without skipping levels for visual styling.

## 11. Paragraphs and lists

- Keep paragraphs focused on one idea.
- Use lists for true sets of checks or options, not to fragment ordinary prose.
- Use complete sentences when an item requires explanation.
- Avoid repeating the heading in the first sentence.
- Remove filler transitions and duplicated conclusions.

## 12. `Our Take`

Use `Our Take` to synthesise evidence and practical trade-offs, not to manufacture an opinion.

It should:

- answer the decision raised by the section;
- state the circumstances in which an option may suit;
- acknowledge meaningful limitations; and
- avoid universal or unqualified recommendations.

Omit it when the article already reaches a clear, concise conclusion.

## 13. Checklists

Every checklist item must be specific and actionable.

Weak:

> Measure the room.

Stronger:

> Measure the narrowest doorway and turn between the delivery vehicle and the final installation position.

Group long checklists by stage—before shopping, delivery, installation and ownership—when that helps readers use them in practice.

## 14. Commonly overlooked considerations

Use neutral, non-blaming language.

Prefer:

- `A commonly overlooked detail is ...`
- `One point that is easy to miss ...`
- `Check this before ordering ...`

Avoid implying that readers are careless or that a delivery or installation team can overcome every physical or safety constraint.

## 15. Practical value

Where appropriate, every substantial guide should contain at least one useful point that cannot be obtained from a product specification alone.

Examples include:

- measuring the delivery route as well as the cavity;
- checking full door, drawer or basket travel;
- allowing access for filters, servicing and replacement;
- planning cable routes before wall mounting; and
- checking whether support and spare parts are available in the reader's location.

Use the [Retail Knowledge Base](../knowledge/retail-knowledge-base.md) as a prompt, not as a substitute for current verification.

## 16. No manufactured expertise

Never imply that Australian Home Collective has:

- tested a product it has not tested;
- owned a product it has not owned;
- inspected a home or installation it has not inspected;
- interviewed a customer who does not exist; or
- verified a claim that remains uncertain.

Rely on verifiable sources, recognised planning principles, clearly identified assumptions and practical considerations. State the basis of first-hand claims when they are used.

## 17. Ownership and after-sales support

Buying guidance should extend beyond the transaction where ownership considerations matter.

Encourage readers to investigate:

- written warranty terms;
- who provides warranty and service support;
- the availability of authorised service in their area;
- likely access to spare parts and consumables;
- maintenance and repair access;
- manuals and support channels; and
- how household needs may change.

Do not make broad claims about a brand's service quality without current, strong and fairly presented evidence.

## 18. Consumer rights

Where relevant, guides may note in neutral terms that products and services bought in Australia may include automatic consumer guarantees under Australian Consumer Law, separate from voluntary warranties.

Do not interpret a reader's legal rights or advise on an individual dispute. Link readers to the ACCC's authoritative [consumer rights and guarantees](https://www.accc.gov.au/consumers/buying-products-and-services/consumer-rights-and-guarantees) information.

Do not offer generic judgements about optional care plans or extended warranties.

## 19. Internal links

Internal links must help readers understand or complete the next part of their decision.

- Use descriptive, natural anchor text.
- Link where context creates a genuine next step.
- Prefer the most relevant guide or hub over several near-duplicate links.
- Avoid arbitrary link-count targets and repeated exact-match anchors.
- Check that reciprocal links make sense within topic clusters.

## 20. External sources and links

Prefer primary, current and authoritative Australian sources for legal, safety, energy, water and regulatory information. Manufacturer documentation may support product-specific requirements.

- Attribute material factual claims.
- Link to the page that supports the claim, not a search result or generic homepage.
- Do not use low-quality sources to manufacture certainty.
- Commercial and affiliate links are governed by disclosure and partnership standards.
- Open external links in a new tab and use `rel="noopener noreferrer"`.
- Keep internal Australian Home Collective links in the same tab.

## 21. Images and diagrams

Images should educate or orient the reader. Prefer:

1. measurement diagrams;
2. planning layouts;
3. comparison graphics;
4. annotated room or product examples; and
5. supporting lifestyle imagery.

Write useful alt text for informative images. Use empty alt text for purely decorative images. Do not repeat nearby captions word for word.

Apply the photography, colour, material variety and physical plausibility requirements in the [Brand Style Guide](./brand-style-guide.md) to every commissioned or generated image.

## 22. Tables

Use tables when they make repeated comparisons easier to understand.

- Give columns clear labels.
- Keep cells concise.
- Ensure the information remains usable on small screens.
- Do not invent scores, ratings or precision unsupported by a transparent method.

## 23. Editorial responsibility

Every article must receive editorial review before publication. Australian Home Collective accepts responsibility for the accuracy, clarity and quality of the final work.

If information cannot be verified, qualify or remove it. If an error is identified, correct it in line with the Corrections & Content Review Policy.

## 24. Affiliate and commercial language

Affiliate links should follow the planning guidance, not replace it.

- Do not publish named model recommendations, retailer links or affiliate links before the commercial workflow is formally approved.
- Explain what readers should measure and compare first.
- Disclose material commercial relationships clearly.
- Do not allow commission rates to determine conclusions.
- Do not create artificial urgency, scarcity or fear.
- Do not disguise advertising as independent editorial.

## 25. Planning tools

Interactive tools must:

- solve a genuine problem;
- explain why important inputs matter;
- validate information safely;
- identify unknowns and limitations;
- avoid false precision and guarantees; and
- distinguish a planning assessment from professional, delivery or installation approval.

## 26. Review standard

Review dates must change only after a genuine editorial review. A review should consider, as relevant:

- factual accuracy;
- current manufacturer or authoritative guidance;
- broken or outdated links;
- practical usefulness;
- readability and accessibility;
- internal linking and metadata; and
- whether new risks or limitations need explanation.

Use `Published Month Year` for a newly published guide. Use `Updated Month Year` only after a substantive change to the reader-facing guidance. Do not advance a date for styling, metadata, image, spelling or formatting work alone. Any `dateModified` value in structured data must match a genuine substantive update.

## 27. The AHC Test

Before publication, ask:

1. Does this solve a real problem?
2. Could it help someone avoid an expensive mistake?
3. Would we confidently recommend it to a customer or family member?
4. Is it likely to remain useful in five years?
5. Does it build trust?

## 28. Golden rule

> **If a decision improves short-term revenue but reduces long-term trust, we do not do it.**
