## 1. Brand position

Australian Home Collective is an independent Australian home-planning publication.

We help Australians make better home-planning and buying decisions through practical guidance, tools and educational resources.

We are not a retailer, review laboratory, deals site, coupon site or general lifestyle magazine. We are a trusted planning resource.

## 2. Brand promise

Australian Home Collective promises:

- practical advice;
- honest explanations;
- independent thinking;
- clear limitations; and
- long-term usefulness.

We do not promise perfect answers, guaranteed outcomes, the lowest prices or a single choice that suits everyone.

## 3. Motto

> **Plan Better. Buy Smarter.**

Use the motto deliberately. It should not be repeated so often that it becomes advertising wallpaper.

## 4. Personality

If Australian Home Collective were a person, it would be:

- practical;
- calm;
- experienced;
- honest;
- helpful;
- curious;
- patient; and
- professional.

It would not be loud, arrogant, sales-driven, sarcastic, sensational or dismissive.

## 5. Reader relationship

Treat readers as capable adults.

We do not lecture, patronise or pressure. We explain relevant details, acknowledge reasonable alternatives and help readers decide what suits their circumstances.

## 6. Voice model

Australian Home Collective should sound like:

> A knowledgeable person explaining what they would want their own family to know before spending money.

It should not sound like a salesperson closing a sale, an academic obscuring a simple point or a marketing team manufacturing excitement.

## 7. Preferred language

Prefer language that connects an option to circumstances:

- `Consider ...`
- `This may suit ...`
- `This works well when ...`
- `Check whether ...`
- `The trade-off is ...`
- `A commonly overlooked detail is ...`

Use direct language where a verified requirement or physical limitation applies.

## 8. Language to avoid

Avoid unsupported or promotional expressions such as:

- best ever;
- game changer;
- revolutionary;
- ultimate;
- guaranteed;
- perfect;
- no-brainer;
- must-have;
- number one; and
- life-changing.

Do not ban ordinary words mechanically. `Must`, for example, is appropriate for a genuine safety or manufacturer requirement.

## 9. What makes AHC distinctive

AHC considers the full path from planning to ownership:

1. Will it suit the home and intended use?
2. Will it fit the space and delivery route?
3. Can it be installed safely and practically?
4. Can it be used, opened, cleaned and maintained?
5. What support may be available after purchase?

Every substantial guide should aim to add something readers would not learn from a product brochure alone.

## 10. Trust language

Avoid certainty that the evidence cannot support.

Instead of `This is the right choice`, explain `This may suit households where ...`.

Instead of `It will fit`, explain `No obvious measurement conflict was identified from the information provided`.

Honest limitations strengthen the brand.

## 11. Naming conventions

- Use `Australian Home Collective` in full on first reference.
- Use `AHC` mainly in internal material; avoid assuming public readers know the abbreviation.
- Use descriptive tool names that state the job, such as `Fridge Fit & Delivery Planner` and `TV Wall-Mount Planner`.
- Use `Planning Centre` as the working name for the collection of tools and resources.
- Do not add trademark symbols or claim proprietary names without appropriate business and legal review.

## 12. Visual identity principles

Visuals should improve understanding. Prioritise diagrams, measurements, layouts and comparison graphics before decorative imagery.

The visual tone should be clear, calm and useful. Avoid badges, warning colours or interface patterns that exaggerate certainty or urgency.

### Photography and generated imagery

Australian Home Collective imagery should feel recognisably varied, contemporary and lived in. Do not allow every room to collapse into the same beige, cream, sage and pale timber look.

- Use confident colour where it suits the subject. Cobalt, teal, wattle, coral, terracotta, burgundy and clear painted finishes can provide useful contrast without making an image loud.
- Vary materials across the site. Painted cabinetry, stainless steel, coloured laminate, terrazzo, tile, stone, upholstery and metal should appear alongside timber rather than timber becoming the default surface in every scene.
- Do not request a `warm timber interior`, `muted neutral palette` or similar stock styling unless the article specifically needs it.
- Give related guides visibly different settings, palettes and compositions. A new image should add range to the site rather than reproduce the dominant look of recent images.
- Keep the product or planning issue easy to read. Colour must not obscure controls, clearances, hazards or the feature the image is meant to explain.
- Check physical plausibility before use. Cords, hoses, doors, handles, controls, reflections, shadows and product placement must make sense. A cordless product must not appear connected to a power lead, and a robot vacuum must not appear tethered to its dock.
- Reject generated images with invented brand marks, unreadable labels, malformed products or ambiguous accessories. Regenerate the image instead of explaining away a visible error in the caption.

Before approving an image, compare it with the most recent images in the same category and across the Guides Index. If the colour palette, timber treatment or room styling feels repetitive, choose a different visual direction.

## 13. Tools and result language

Tools should reduce uncertainty while making unknowns visible.

Prefer result labels such as:

- `No obvious issue identified`;
- `Further checks recommended`;
- `Potential concern identified`; and
- `Professional confirmation recommended`.

Avoid `Guaranteed`, `Approved`, `Certified` and unqualified `Pass` or `Safe` results.

## 14. Publication test

Before publishing, ask whether the work strengthens Australian Home Collective's reputation. If it does not, revise it or do not publish it.

## 15. Reader test

The desired recommendation is not:

> They told me to buy Brand X.

It is:

> They helped me avoid an expensive mistake.

## 16. Reputation test

Assume Australian Home Collective may one day be asked publicly to explain or defend any published claim. Publish only work the business can stand behind.

## 17. Growth rule

Measure growth using reader trust, returning visitors, usefulness, quality and reputation—not article count, page views, affiliate clicks or advertising impressions alone.
